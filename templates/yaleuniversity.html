Yale University Financial Aid Principles

When awarding aid, we are guided by the following principles:

  • Admission is need-blind for all applicants, including international students. Applying for aid does not affect admission decisions, promoting equitable access for students of diverse financial backgrounds.
  • Yale financial aid is awarded based on need alone; there are no merit-based scholarships. Academic or extracurricular accomplishments are considered only for admission, not financial aid.
  • Each family's financial situation is reviewed comprehensively to assess need. Yale provides personalized consideration for special financial circumstances, using professional judgment.
  • Yale meets 100% of demonstrated financial need for all admitted students through grants and scholarships, without including loans in aid packages.

What Yale Looks for in Applicants

Many years ago, former Yale President Kingman Brewster emphasized the qualities Yale seeks in its applicants: those who would make the most of the university's resources, show a zest for stretching their talents, and demonstrate outstanding public motivation. Today, Yale continues to seek students who will become leaders in their generation, sharing a dedication to improving the world. The admissions process remains guided by two main questions: “Who is likely to make the most of Yale’s resources?” and “Who will contribute most significantly to the Yale community?”

Key Qualities Yale Looks For

  • 📚Academic Ability: Yale places a strong emphasis on academic strength, with the high school transcript being the most critical document. It should reflect a history of challenging courses and strong performance.
  • 💬Teacher and Counselor Recommendations: Recommendations provide insights into a student’s intellectual curiosity, relationships with peers, energy, and overall impact in the classroom. It's important to request recommendations from teachers who know you well.
  • 🎓Leadership and Community Engagement: Applicants should demonstrate leadership skills, active community involvement, and a desire to make a positive difference.
  • 📝Authentic Essays: Essays should be genuine, showcasing a student’s voice and personal experiences. Yale values honest writing that reflects who the applicant is beyond their accomplishments.
  • 🌍Overcoming Challenges: Yale looks for students who have faced challenges, learned from them, and emerged stronger, showing resilience and growth.

No Score Cutoffs

Yale evaluates standardized test scores in context, considering them alongside transcripts and other academic indicators. While there are no cutoffs, the middle 80% ranges of scores for admitted students in 2020 were:

  • ACT Composite: 31-36
  • SAT Evidence-Based Reading and Writing: 680-790
  • SAT Math: 690-800

These ranges are not strict benchmarks but rather a snapshot of enrolled students’ scores. Scores below these ranges can still reflect academic readiness when assessed within a student's unique context.

Bringing Your Application to Life

Yale’s application process seeks to understand the whole student. Teacher and counselor recommendations offer a glimpse into your contributions in the classroom and school community. The application essays and other materials allow applicants to express their personal stories and academic interests in their own voice. Yale admissions officers look for qualities such as:

  • Intellectual Curiosity and the ability to engage deeply with academic topics.
  • Engagement in Extracurriculars that demonstrate leadership, creativity, or dedication.
  • Commitment to Diversity and Inclusion, showing respect for different perspectives and experiences.
  • Balance and Well-being, reflecting on how you prioritize your mental and physical health.

Evaluating Applications

Yale’s admissions committee, comprising experienced officers, faculty, and deans, reviews applications to identify exceptional talent and promise. The committee evaluates transcripts, test scores, essays, and recommendations to understand not only a student’s past achievements but also their potential future impact at Yale and beyond. Context is key: Yale considers the opportunities available to each applicant, such as access to AP or IB courses, and evaluates how they have engaged with those resources.

Kingman Brewster’s belief that students who strive to push their limits have an advantage still holds true: Yale seeks students ready to make the most of what the university offers and stretch their potential to the fullest.

Tips for Yale Applicants

  • Pursue What You Love: Follow your passions and showcase them in your application.
  • Be Authentic: Write essays that reflect your genuine voice and experiences.
  • Select Recommenders Wisely: Choose teachers who can speak to your strengths and character.
  • Apply and Relax: Trust that a variety of factors come together in the admissions decision, so focus on presenting your best self.

Yale’s admissions process values every component of the application. The aim is to find students who not only thrive academically but also contribute meaningfully to the Yale community and the world beyond.

Yale's Supplemental Essay Prompts

Prompt #1: Academic Interests

Students at Yale have time to explore their academic interests before committing to one or more major fields of study. Many students either modify their original academic direction or change their minds entirely. As of this moment, what academic areas seem to fit your interests or goals most comfortably? Please indicate up to three from the list provided.

Tell us about a topic or idea that excites you and is related to one or more academic areas you selected above. Why are you drawn to it? (200 words or fewer)

Prompt #2: "Why Yale?"

What is it about Yale that has led you to apply? (125 words or fewer)

Prompt #3: Inspiration

What inspires you?

Prompt #4: Creative Expression

If you could teach any college course, write a book, or create an original piece of art of any kind, what would it be?

Prompt #5: Influences

Other than a family member, who is someone who has had a significant influence on you? What has been the impact of their influence?

Prompt #6: Additional Information

What is something about you that is not included anywhere else in your application?

Essay Prompts

Applicants submitting the Coalition Application or Common Application will respond to one of the following prompts in 400 words or fewer.

  1. Prompt 1: Reflect on a time you discussed an issue important to you with someone holding an opposing view. Why did you find the experience meaningful?
  2. Prompt 2: Reflect on your membership in a community to which you feel connected. Why is this community meaningful to you? You may define community however you like.
  3. Prompt 3: Reflect on an element of your personal experience that you feel will enrich your college. How has it shaped you?
