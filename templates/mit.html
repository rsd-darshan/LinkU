What MIT Looks for in Applicants

At MIT, we believe that a successful applicant embodies a strong alignment with our mission to apply knowledge to solve real-world problems. While academic excellence is important, the Institute looks for students who exhibit a collaborative spirit, intellectual curiosity, and a willingness to take risks in pursuit of knowledge. MIT values students who actively seek out opportunities to innovate and who are passionate about contributing to the community and society at large.

We are not looking for applicants to have achieved perfection but to show a genuine passion for learning, taking initiative, and engaging with challenges. The match between you and MIT—your curiosity, creativity, and collaborative mindset—is what drives our selection process. We value how you contribute to your community and how your personal values align with our commitment to making the world a better place.

Qualities MIT Values in Applicants

  • 📚Academic Excellence: MIT looks for students who are committed to rigorous academic achievement and who thrive in a challenging environment. A strong academic record, paired with a passion for problem-solving, is key.
  • 💡Intellectual Curiosity: We value applicants who are driven by curiosity, eager to explore new ideas, and excited to tackle complex, interdisciplinary challenges.
  • 🚀Initiative and Risk-Taking: MIT seeks students who seize opportunities and take risks, understanding that failure is part of the learning process. Taking initiative and applying creativity to challenges is essential.
  • 📈Resilience and Growth: Students who demonstrate resilience—those who learn from failure and persist—are particularly valued. MIT appreciates applicants who have shown growth through overcoming challenges.
  • 🎨Hands-On Creativity: We seek applicants who apply theoretical knowledge in practical, real-world ways. Hands-on projects, experimentation, and creative solutions are central to the MIT experience.
  • 🤝Collaborative and Cooperative Spirit: MIT thrives on collaboration. We look for students who work well in teams, embrace diversity of thought, and foster a cooperative, inclusive environment.
  • 🏆Commitment to Community: MIT values students who are responsible, ethical, and committed to making a positive impact on their communities. A strong sense of civic duty is key to contributing to MIT’s mission.

How to Demonstrate Your Fit with MIT

Consider how you can illustrate your alignment with MIT’s mission and core values through your application:

  • How do your goals and experiences align with MIT’s mission to apply knowledge to solve real-world problems?
  • What experiences demonstrate your ability to work collaboratively, especially in interdisciplinary settings?
  • How have you shown initiative in your academic, personal, or extracurricular life?
  • How have you taken risks and learned from failure? How have you shown resilience in the face of challenges?
  • In what ways have you demonstrated hands-on creativity and innovation, especially in problem-solving?
  • How do you contribute to the communities you’re part of? How will you contribute to MIT’s collaborative environment?
  • How do you manage time and balance priorities in a way that will allow you to thrive at MIT while making time for personal growth and relaxation?

Suggestions to Get Into MIT

  • 📖Embrace Your Unique Qualities: Highlight your unique perspective, talents, and experiences that align with MIT’s values, especially in the context of collaboration, innovation, and resilience.
  • 📚Commit to Academic Excellence: Showcase your academic rigor, curiosity, and the intellectual challenges you’ve tackled, especially if they demonstrate problem-solving and creative thinking.
  • 💡Show Initiative and Risk-Taking: Provide examples of how you’ve taken initiative, pursued independent projects, and shown a willingness to take calculated risks in pursuit of new ideas and goals.
  • 🚀Be Hands-On and Creative: Engage in projects that demonstrate your ability to apply knowledge to real-world problems. MIT values students who take a practical, hands-on approach to innovation.
  • 📈Show Growth and Resilience: Reflect on times you faced setbacks and how you overcame them, demonstrating your ability to bounce back and learn from challenges.
  • 🎨Demonstrate Interdisciplinary Thinking: Highlight how you have worked across disciplines and embraced a creative, interdisciplinary approach to problem-solving.
  • 🤝Work Well with Others: Show how you’ve contributed to collaborative efforts, from school projects to community initiatives, and how you’ve fostered inclusivity in these settings.
  • 🏆Contribute to MIT’s Community: Illustrate how your unique background will enrich MIT’s diverse community and how you will engage with others in meaningful ways.
  • ✍️Write Reflective Essays: Use your essays to demonstrate your alignment with MIT’s values. Reflect on how your experiences and goals fit with MIT’s mission to make the world a better place.
  • 📆Prioritize Balance: Show that you know how to manage time effectively, balancing academic rigor with personal well-being and downtime to succeed at MIT.

MIT's Supplemental Essay Prompts

Prompt #1

What field of study appeals to you the most right now? (Note: Applicants select from a drop-down list.) Tell us more about why this field of study at MIT appeals to you. (100 words or fewer)

My decision to major in Electrical Engineering was inspired by my desire to improve security through technology. When I lived in Mexico, my father’s restaurant security system lacked the ability to protect our property from robbers, who would break in multiple times a year. Thanks to the influence of my cousin, who now studies Autonomous Systems, I developed an interest in electrical engineering. I am inspired to not only improve my father’s security system, but contributing to security innovations for larger companies and perhaps, one day, national security.

Source: College Essay Guy

Prompt #2

We know you lead a busy life, full of activities, many of which are required of you. Tell us about something you do simply for the pleasure of it. (150 words or fewer)

It’s impossible to get the perfect tapioca-ball-to-milk-tea ratio. Every bubble tea leaves boba pearls orphaned at the bottom of my cup. Small orbs of black, chewy satisfaction. Some are tucked under ice cubes, as I maneuver the straw to consume the stowaways. I take a breath from every day’s chaos to focus on the (literal) little things. When I finally pin the last pearl under the trap of my straw, it’s like finding the last piece of a puzzle: a playful desire for completion and a cause to celebrate. It’s a sugar-high moment to commemorate the 5-year-old inside.

Source: Sarah Chieng

Prompt #3

While some reach their goals following well-trodden paths, others blaze their own trails achieving the unexpected. In what ways have you done something different than what was expected in your educational journey?

Growing up, I didn’t understand the satisfaction behind my parents’ 9-5 computer science job. Consequently, I made the poorly-grounded resolution to look everywhere - but computer science - for a passion.

It worked. Years of biology groundwork culminated into a grand moment. I stood in a room full of professors, graduates, and parents, presenting a powerpoint on my glioblastoma cell research. In 15 slides, the last six weeks of frustration, support, and maturation came alive. I had found a path and a talent: biochemistry.

Simultaneously, influenced by a natural curiosity, I decided it was now-or-never to try computer science. I took AP Computer Science and competed in the Science Olympiad event, “Game On.” I overcame my rudimentary computer science experience and found myself appreciating the class and loving the event. I stood on the MIT Science Olympiad stage and one month later the UPenn stage, realizing a future in computer science wasn’t such a stretch. I discovered a second calling.

Since I can remember, I’ve been told to pick a direction and stick to it. I tried. But years later, I realized how this expectation to isolate “a” passion was unrealistic. From biochemistry to computer science, I’ve never enjoyed a one-track path. I have a foundation of experience and opportunities I cherish, because ultimately, I grew to become open-minded. Immersed in the diversity of the MIT campus, community, and courses, I aspire to preserve this mindset.

Source: Sarah Chieng

Prompt #4

MIT brings people with diverse backgrounds together to collaborate, from tackling the world’s biggest challenges to lending a helping hand. Describe one way you have collaborated with others to learn from them, with them, or contribute to your community together.

From a young age, teaching has been my way to give back to my community, whether it’s my immediate family or neighbors on the opposite edge of London.

My first teaching experience took place when I was 10. My five-year-old sister took a principled moral stand against homework by getting under the table and refusing to do her math addition tables. After almost an hour of negotiations, our mother admitted defeat, so I jumped in to assist her. My sister and I worked patiently through the tables, finding new tricks to help her master the relationship between the numbers. And I found myself genuinely thrilled to have helped her — and my mother — in that difficult moment.

I have been teaching regularly ever since. At Imperial College’s programming club, I volunteered to help show young people the creative side of coding through graphical programming. At people’s homes, I have adapted to people’s needs, from teaching Computer GCSE to helping a group of four girls make a moving Raspberry Pi robot. And during lockdown, I offered free Python coding classes to kids of essential workers. In each, I not only provided support to students to complete their stated assignments, but I also found myself learning from the students along the way. This process helped me discover novel solutions to my own challenges as a programmer.

It is this two-way exchange between teacher and student that I find so rewarding in this contribution to my community. Teaching has allowed me to learn with others, contribute to their growth, and expand my own understanding of subjects I love. This experience of collaboration motivates me to continue working alongside others, just as I would at MIT, where I can both teach and learn in a vibrant, diverse environment.

Source: College Essay Guy

Prompt #5

How did you manage a situation or challenge that you didn’t expect? What did you learn from it?

I was standing on the top row of the choir risers with my fellow third graders. We were beside the fourth graders who were beside the fifth graders. My teacher struck the first chords of our favorite song and we sang together, in proud call and response “Ujima, let us work together. To make better our community. We can solve! Solve our problems with collective work and responsibility.”

Then the students playing African drums and the xylophones on the floor began the harmonious percussion section and we sang again with as much passion as nine-year-olds can muster. This was my world. As a child, my community was centered around my school. At my school we discovered that if you love something enough, and work hard enough for it, you can do great things for both yourself and others around you.

In the years since I left, I reflected back on the lessons I learned at school. I determined I wanted to focus on the things I love – mathematics, science, and helping others. I also want to harmonize my abilities with those of other people so that we can work together to make the world a better place. Today I aspire to work in integrative research as a bioengineer to address the pressing medical issues of today.

Source: CollegeAdvisor
